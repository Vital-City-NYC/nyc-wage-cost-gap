The prosperity equation

The wage–cost gap, and why rents go unpaid

Productivity has climbed steadily since the 1970s. Wages for the bottom half of American earners have not. In New York City, that long divergence is showing up at the rent ledger: roughly six in ten New Yorkers are economically insecure, and rent collection in affordable housing has been sliding since 2017.

Methodology

Source: Abby Jo Sigal and Molly Wasow Park for Vital City. Productivity-vs-wage divergence: Economic Policy Institute analysis of U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics productivity series and Current Population Survey real wages for the bottom 50% of earners, indexed to 1973 = 100. Rent collection: industry data on Low Income Housing Tax Credit and other regulated affordable-housing portfolios in New York City, 2017 and most recent year cited in the essay. Economic insecurity and "good job" share: New York City Comptroller, The State of Job Quality in New York City.

How to read this: The productivity line is total nonfarm output per hour worked. The wage line tracks median real hourly earnings for the bottom 50% of earners, in 2024 dollars. The rent-collection panel compares the 2017 baseline with the most recent industry-reported figure; ">3×" is the essay's characterization of the share of projects that have crossed below the 80% collection threshold.

Limitations: The productivity-wage divergence is well documented but its magnitude depends on which deflator is used. Rent-collection figures vary across operators and reporting frameworks; treat the levels as illustrative of direction, not as official audit results.
