The prosperity equation

The wage–cost gap, and why rents go unpaid

Productivity has climbed steadily since the 1970s. Wages for the bottom half of American earners have not. In New York City, that long divergence is showing up at the rent ledger: roughly six in ten New Yorkers are economically insecure, and rent collection in affordable housing has been sliding since 2017.

Methodology

Source: Productivity-vs-wage divergence: Economic Policy Institute analysis of U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics labor productivity and Current Population Survey real wages for the bottom 50% of earners, indexed to 1973 = 100. Rent collection: industry data on Low-Income Housing Tax Credit and other regulated affordable-housing portfolios in New York City, 2017 and most recent year cited in the essay. Economic insecurity and "good job" share: New York City Comptroller, The State of Job Quality in New York City.

How to read this: The two figures come directly from Sigal and Park's essay. "−~5 pts" reflects the authors' statement that "rent collection in affordable housing has dropped almost five percentage points since 2017"; the absolute starting and ending levels are not specified. "More than tripled" describes the share of regulated affordable-housing projects in New York City with rent-collection rates below 80%, also relative to 2017.

Limitations: Rent-collection figures vary across operators and reporting frameworks; treat the changes as the authors' characterization of trend direction, not as audited industry totals. The 1973 productivity-vs-wage divergence cited in the essay is well documented in U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics and Economic Policy Institute data but is not depicted here because the authors do not specify magnitudes.
